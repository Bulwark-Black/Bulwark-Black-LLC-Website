<p>Adidas has confirmed it is actively investigating a data breach at one of its independent licensing partners after threat actors claiming to be the Lapsus$ Group posted allegedly stolen data on BreachForums. The incident highlights the ongoing risks posed by third-party vendor relationships in enterprise supply chains.</p>
<h2>What Happened</h2>
<p>On February 16, 2026, someone purporting to represent the notorious Lapsus$ Group posted on BreachForums claiming to have compromised Adidas&#8217;s extranet. According to the <a href="https://www.theregister.com/2026/02/18/adidas_investigates_thirdparty_data_breach/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">original report from The Register</a>, the alleged stolen dataset contains <strong>815,000 rows of information</strong> including:</p>
<ul>
<li>First and last names</li>
<li>Email addresses</li>
<li>Passwords</li>
<li>Birthdays</li>
<li>Company names</li>
<li>&#8220;A lot of technical data&#8221;</li>
</ul>
<h2>Adidas Response</h2>
<p>An Adidas spokesperson confirmed to The Register that the company is investigating a &#8220;potential data protection incident&#8221; at one of their independent licensing partners and distributors for martial arts products. Critically, Adidas emphasized that this is an independent company operating its own IT systems.</p>
<p>The spokesperson stated there is <strong>&#8220;no indication that the Adidas IT infrastructure, our own e-commerce platforms, or any of our consumer data are affected by the incident.&#8221;</strong> However, the company declined to specify when the compromise occurred or what specific information may have been stolen.</p>
<h2>Pattern of Third-Party Breaches</h2>
<p>This incident marks the second third-party security breach affecting Adidas in less than a year. In May 2025, the sportswear giant notified customers that data was stolen from a third-party customer service provider after an unauthorized intrusion.</p>
<p>The recurring pattern underscores a critical vulnerability facing large enterprises: their security is only as strong as their weakest vendor. Third-party risk management has become one of the most challenging aspects of enterprise security programs.</p>
<h2>Who is Lapsus$?</h2>
<p>Lapsus$ is a chaotic cybercrime group composed primarily of teenagers and young adults who gained notoriety during a prolific 2021-2022 crime spree. The gang successfully breached high-profile targets including:</p>
<ul>
<li>BT (British Telecom)</li>
<li>Nvidia</li>
<li>Microsoft</li>
<li>Samsung</li>
<li>Vodafone</li>
<li>Revolut</li>
<li>Okta</li>
</ul>
<p>The group employed creative attack methods including phone-based social engineering, SIM swapping, and even paying employees of target organizations for access to credentials and multi-factor authentication codes.</p>
<p>In March 2022, UK police arrested seven individuals aged 16-21 for their alleged involvement. Two were subsequently charged.</p>
<h2>The Scattered Lapsus$ Hunters Connection</h2>
<p>In August 2025, Lapsus$ members allegedly joined forces with two other notorious cybercrime groups—Scattered Spider and ShinyHunters—forming a collective called &#8220;Scattered Lapsus$ Hunters.&#8221; This collaboration of some of the most effective cybercriminal crews represents a significant threat escalation.</p>
<p>In October 2025, this extortion collective listed Adidas on its leak site, claiming to have stolen over 20 million sensitive records in a February 2024 breach—suggesting the sportswear giant may have been a persistent target.</p>
<h2>Implications for Organizations</h2>
<p>This breach reinforces several critical lessons for security teams:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Third-party risk is first-party risk:</strong> Your security posture includes every vendor, partner, and contractor with access to your data</li>
<li><strong>Supply chain attacks continue to pay off:</strong> Threat actors increasingly target smaller partners to reach larger organizations</li>
<li><strong>Threat actor collaboration is increasing:</strong> Groups like Scattered Lapsus$ Hunters demonstrate that cybercriminals are pooling resources and expertise</li>
<li><strong>Data compartmentalization matters:</strong> Adidas&#8217;s architecture apparently limited the breach to a partner&#8217;s systems—a security win</li>
</ul>
