<p>Palo Alto Networks&#8217; Unit 42 has released their <a href="https://www.paloaltonetworks.com/blog/2026/02/unit-42-global-ir-report/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">2026 Global Incident Response Report</a>, analyzing over 750 major cyber incidents across 50+ countries. The findings paint a stark picture of an evolving threat landscape where attacks are faster, broader, and harder to contain than ever before.</p>
<h2>Key Finding: 72-Minute Attack Chains</h2>
<p>In the fastest cases Unit 42 investigated, attackers needed <strong>just 72 minutes to move from initial access to data exfiltration</strong>—four times faster than observed last year. This dramatic compression of attack timelines is driven by AI being used in reconnaissance, phishing, scripting, and operational execution, enabling machine-like speed at scale.</p>
<h2>Identity: The Primary Attack Vector</h2>
<p>Identity weaknesses played a material role in <strong>nearly 90% of investigations</strong>. Attackers are increasingly &#8220;logging in, not breaking in&#8221;—leveraging stolen credentials and tokens to exploit fragmented identity estates, escalate privileges, and move laterally without triggering traditional defenses.</p>
<h2>Supply Chain Risk Drives Operational Disruption</h2>
<p>In <strong>23% of incidents</strong>, attackers leveraged third-party SaaS applications. By abusing trusted integrations, vendor tools, and application dependencies, threat actors bypassed traditional perimeters and expanded impact well beyond single systems.</p>
<h2>Additional Critical Findings</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Multi-Surface Attacks:</strong> 87% of intrusions involved activity across multiple attack surfaces (endpoints, networks, cloud, SaaS, and identity)</li>
<li><strong>Browser Battleground:</strong> Nearly 48% of incidents included browser-based activity, reflecting how modern attacks intersect with routine workflows</li>
<li><strong>Extortion Evolution:</strong> Encryption-based extortion declined 15%, as more attackers skip encryption and move straight to data theft and disruption—a faster, quieter approach that creates immediate pressure</li>
</ul>
<h2>Why Attacks Still Succeed</h2>
<p>Despite increasing sophistication, most incidents Unit 42 responds to don&#8217;t start with something radically new—they start with preventable gaps:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Environmental Complexity:</strong> Over 90% of incidents were materially enabled by misconfigurations or gaps in security coverage. Many organizations run 50+ security products, making consistent control deployment extremely difficult.</li>
<li><strong>Visibility Gaps:</strong> During attacks, teams often had to stitch together data from multiple disconnected sources, slowing detection during critical early minutes.</li>
<li><strong>Excessive Trust:</strong> Overly permissive access and unmanaged tokens frequently allowed attackers to move farther than they should after gaining initial footholds.</li>
</ul>
<h2>Recommendations for Defenders</h2>
<p>Unit 42&#8217;s recommendations for security leaders focus on three priorities:</p>
<ol>
<li><strong>Reduce Exposure:</strong> Secure the full application ecosystem and treat trusted connections with the same scrutiny as core infrastructure.</li>
<li><strong>Reduce Area of Impact:</strong> Tighten identity and access management while removing unnecessary trust to limit attacker lateral movement.</li>
<li><strong>Increase Response Speed:</strong> Ensure visibility across environments and leverage AI to detect, identify, and prioritize threats—enabling containment at machine speed, faster than adversaries can move.</li>
</ol>
<h2>Why This Matters</h2>
<p>This report underscores a fundamental shift in cybersecurity: the window between initial access and business impact is shrinking dramatically. Organizations must adapt their defenses to match the speed of modern AI-assisted attacks while addressing the persistent foundational gaps—complexity, limited visibility, and excessive trust—that attackers continue to exploit.</p>
