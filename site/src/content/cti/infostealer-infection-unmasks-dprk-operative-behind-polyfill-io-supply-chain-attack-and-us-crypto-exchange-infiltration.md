<p>In a stunning example of operational security failure, a North Korean cyber operative was unmasked after infecting their own machine with a <strong>LummaC2 infostealer</strong>&mdash;revealing definitive evidence linking them to both the catastrophic <strong>Polyfill.io supply chain attack</strong> and deep infiltration of a US cryptocurrency exchange.</p>
<h2>Key Findings</h2>
<p>According to a detailed forensic analysis by <a href="https://www.infostealers.com/article/how-one-infostealer-infection-solved-a-global-supply-chain-mystery-and-unmasked-dprk-spies-in-u-s-crypto/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Hudson Rock researchers</a>, the DPRK operative made a fatal mistake: downloading a fake software installer that delivered a LummaC2 infostealer sample. The malware exfiltrated over <strong>100 credentials, 7,000+ browsing logs, direct Cloudflare admin access</strong>, and thousands of internal Google Translate records that definitively identified the operator&#8217;s native language as Korean.</p>
<h2>Why This Matters</h2>
<p>This investigation reattributes one of 2024&#8217;s most significant supply chain attacks. While the <strong>Polyfill.io compromise</strong>&mdash;which affected over 100,000 websites&mdash;was initially attributed to Chinese threat actors operating through the &#8220;Funnull&#8221; CDN company, the forensic evidence now conclusively links the operation to North Korean state-sponsored actors embedded within the Chinese syndicate.</p>
<h2>The Polyfill.io Connection</h2>
<p>The credential dumps extracted by the infostealer contained:</p>
<ul>
<li>Direct developer credentials to Funnull&#8217;s DNS management portal</li>
<li>Master credentials for the <code>[[email protected]]</code> Cloudflare tenant</li>
<li>Google Translate telemetry capturing real-time discussion of modifying Polyfill domains during the attack</li>
<li>Internal communications showing Chinese handlers directing the North Korean coder to hide malicious code inside the &#8220;GoEdge&#8221; CDN build process</li>
</ul>
<h2>Gate.us Infiltration: Inside the Compliance Team</h2>
<p>Perhaps the most alarming discovery: the same DPRK operative had successfully infiltrated <strong>Gate.us</strong>, an American cryptocurrency exchange, under the synthetic persona &#8220;Ariel Cruz.&#8221; The operative:</p>
<ul>
<li>Participated in Google Meet calls with Western compliance vendors (Sumsub) to help define AML/KYC logic</li>
<li>Intercepted and translated executive communications about biometric data liability</li>
<li>Exfiltrated internal architecture diagrams showing KYC data routing</li>
<li>Tested the staging environment using profiles of real FBI fugitives including Bernard Madoff and George Wright</li>
</ul>
<h2>Operational Security Failures</h2>
<p>The investigation exposed multiple OPSEC failures:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>&#8220;Mental Bridge&#8221; workflow:</strong> Massive Google Translate telemetry revealed the operative translating English (from US employers) and Chinese (from Funnull handlers) into Korean to comprehend them</li>
<li><strong>Timezone slip:</strong> After crafting an excuse to miss a US meeting, the operative immediately referenced &#8220;8 pm Beijing time&#8221; in messages to their handler</li>
<li><strong>Password recycling:</strong> Distinct password tiers linked disparate accounts to a single operator</li>
<li><strong>Stark Industries C2:</strong> A password prefixed with &#8220;nk&#8221; (likely &#8220;North Korea&#8221;) was isolated for access to Russian bulletproof hosting infrastructure</li>
</ul>
<h2>Japanese Scientific Espionage</h2>
<p>Beyond revenue generation, the logs revealed the operative exfiltrated <strong>air-gapped network blueprints</strong> from Japan&#8217;s National Institute for Materials Science (NIMS)&mdash;demonstrating a pivot from IT wage theft to strategic state espionage targeting critical infrastructure.</p>
<h2>The Performance Improvement Plan</h2>
<p>In an ironic twist, internal communications revealed the North Korean operative was placed on a &#8220;Performance Improvement Plan&#8221; by their Chinese handlers and given a salary cut to $3,000/month for needing &#8220;too much guidance&#8221;&mdash;despite orchestrating massive cyber warfare campaigns.</p>
<h2>Implications for Defenders</h2>
<p>This case demonstrates:</p>
<ul>
<li>The convergence of DPRK IT worker programs with advanced supply chain attacks</li>
<li>How insider threats can compromise compliance systems from within</li>
<li>The intelligence value of infostealer telemetry for attribution</li>
<li>The need for enhanced vetting of remote IT workers, especially in crypto/fintech</li>
</ul>
